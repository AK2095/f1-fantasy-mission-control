About

What this is, how it works, and where every number comes from

What this is

A decision dashboard for F1 Fantasy. It exists to answer one question well: what should my team be before this weekend's lock, and why?

Fantasy decisions are made in the days before qualifying closes the squad — which driver to boost, when to spend a chip, whether to chase a rival or protect a lead. The information needed to make those calls well is spread across half a dozen sites, and the signals that matter most aren't published anywhere at all. They have to be computed.

Why it was built

Checking a fantasy site, a results site, a weather service, and a prediction market separately — then trying to hold it all in your head — is slow and easy to get wrong. Worse, the interesting questions can't be answered by reading any one of them. Which cheap driver actually earns his price? Which asset scores consistently rather than riding one big weekend? Given a fixed budget, what is the best possible team, not just a good one?

This pulls the sources into one place and derives what's missing. Every figure is either fetched from a named source or entered by hand, and the page says which — so it's always clear what is measured and what is inferred.

How it stays current

A scheduled job fetches every automated source, writes the results into the project's repository as plain data files, and the site republishes from that commit. There is no database and no server: the published page carries its own data, and every refresh is recorded as a timestamped change.

Sources are fetched independently, so one being unavailable never blocks the others — the table below reports exactly which are healthy. If the data ever falls behind the current round, the dashboard says so at the top of the page rather than quietly showing old numbers.

Limitations worth knowing

  • Projections are season averages, not race predictions. They describe how an asset has typically scored, which is a reasonable prior but blind to this weekend's specifics — a circuit that suits one car, an upgrade package, a grid penalty.
  • League standings are entered by hand. F1 Fantasy publishes no publicly available interface, so those figures are as current as the last manual update, shown in the table above.
  • Data refreshes on a schedule, not continuously. Figures are current as of the last run rather than this instant. Since squads lock before qualifying, that is early enough for every decision this dashboard supports.
  • Market probabilities are prices, not forecasts. They reflect what people are willing to bet, which is informative but not authoritative.
